## Overview

The **Dragon Curve** (also called the Heighway Dragon, after NASA physicist
John Heighway who first studied it in the 1960s) is a self-similar fractal that can be
constructed by repeatedly folding a strip of paper in half and unfolding it so each fold
is at a right angle.

As an IFS, the Dragon Curve is the attractor of two affine contractions, each a rotation
by ±45° combined with a scaling by 1/√2. The resulting curve is continuous, non-self-intersecting,
and tiles the plane.

## Definition

The Dragon Curve is the attractor of two rotational contractions, both scaling by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.
In the algebraic form the IFS lives in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (the Gaussian integers). The companion matrix
$s$ (defined as `$companion([1,0])` in AIFS) satisfies $s^2 + 1 = 0$, so $s \leftrightarrow i$ in the projected plane.

The expansion $g = s + 1 \leftrightarrow 1+i$ has $|g| = \sqrt{2}$, so $g^{-1}$ contracts by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.

| Map | AIFS | Complex form | Effect |
|-----|------|-------------|--------|
| $f_1$ | `s*g^-1` | $\frac{i}{1+i} = \frac{1+i}{2}$ | scale $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, rotate $+45°$, no translation |
| $f_2$ | `[1,0]*s^3*g^-1` | $-\frac{1+i}{2}\,z + 1$ | scale $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, rotate $+135°$, translate $(1,0)$ |

Here $s^3 = -s \leftrightarrow -i$ (since $s^2 = -1$). The identity $s \cdot g^{-1} = \frac{i}{1+i} = \frac{1+i}{2}$ and
$s^3 \cdot g^{-1} = \frac{-i}{1+i} = \frac{-1-i}{2}$ can be verified directly; only integer matrices
and one matrix inversion are needed — no floating-point values.

## Properties

- **Hausdorff dimension:** 2 (the attractor has positive area and fills a region of the plane)
- **Contractivity ratio:** 1/√2 for both mappings
- **Number of transformations:** 2
- **Tiling:** Four Dragon Curves together tile the plane
- **Boundary:** The curve is nowhere differentiable
- **Constructed by:** John Heighway, NASA (1960s); popularized by Martin Gardner
